Team Model · Human-Centred AI Mimecast · 2020–2025

From service team
to AI-first guild

How a design function restructured itself to stay relevant in an AI-first organisation

Role User Experience Director
Scope Team model, AI tooling, cross-functional practice
Team 5 designers (1 principal), 2 researchers, accessibility lead

01 The journey

When Graham joined Mimecast in 2020, design was a peripheral function. The organisation was engineering and product-led, delivery mattered more than design quality, and designers were frequently either left out of user experience decisions entirely or brought in for low-value work after the important calls had been made.

Over two years that changed. The team built credibility through evidence and outcome, and demand grew. But the organisation had a structural problem that compounded the bottleneck: many teams were operating in a heavily Jira-driven waterfall model. Everything had to be well described, well ticketed, and well understood before work could begin. That discipline had its place, but it made the organisation slow to make decisions and resistant to more flexible, exploratory ways of working. Design sat outside those processes, so even when designers were available and engaged, the sequential hand-offs meant things moved slowly or stalled entirely. AI would later force the organisation's hand on this, demonstrating in practice that faster, more adaptive ways of working were not only possible but necessary. The guild became one of the places where that shift was modelled.

The solution was not to hire more designers. It was to change how the whole system worked.

2020–2022: The problem

Centralised service into waterfall teams

Design resource coordinated and allocated by Graham across the organisation. Demand grew faster than capacity. Waterfall processes in product teams created sequential bottlenecks. Things did not get done, or did not get done at the pace the business needed.

→
2023 onwards: The shift

Embedded squad model with guild as connective tissue

Five designers embedded as leaders within cross-functional product squads. Teams organising their own time, solving problems together, without the hand-off delays of sequential process. Guild providing shared standards, learning, and AI experimentation across the wider organisation.

02 The structural decision

The decision to restructure was Graham's. His view, grounded in the empowered product model associated with Marty Cagan's work, has always been that good user experience only happens in cross-functional teams where designers, engineers, and product managers work as genuine partners. A centralised service model, however well managed, prevents that kind of collaboration from taking root.

Graham spoke to every product leader individually, agreed the line management changes, and then met with each designer to explain what was shifting. They were not being handed off. They were being recognised as design leaders for their product areas, with the authority and proximity to engineering and product that genuine collaboration requires.

The principal designer took on coordination of Mimecast Elements, the shared design system, giving the system dedicated leadership and a clear home within the new structure.

The measure of a mature design function is not how well it manages demand. It is whether design thinking can spread across the organisation without losing coherence.

The guild was the answer to that second question. With designers embedded in squads, the risk was fragmentation: five designers working in isolation, drifting from shared standards, losing the cross-team perspective that makes a design function more than the sum of its parts. The guild provided the connective tissue, and critically, it was open to anyone in the organisation who had an interest in improving the customer experience. Product managers, engineers, customer-facing teams, researchers from pricing and marketing: membership was deliberately broad.

03 AI adoption

How it started

Mimecast was moving to an AI-first operating model. You.com was the first tool adopted across the business, chosen because it aggregates multiple large language models and selects the most appropriate one for each prompt. It came with structured training courses that built genuine understanding of what AI could offer each person in their role. This seeded awareness across the workforce before the design team had worked out how to use AI well in practice.

The guild created the space to move from awareness to experiment. The approach was deliberately exploratory: new technology, new capability, and a need to learn the real lessons about what worked and what did not. Graham encouraged the team and the wider guild to try things, report back, and share what they found. That learning would shape how the guild evolved.

The Cursor training that became a landmark moment for the team started with a lunch conversation between Graham and a lead engineer about AI tooling. A week later the engineer came back with a proposal to run Cursor training for designers. Graham opened the guild Slack channel to the engineering team and encouraged designers to attend. The session demystified the path from design to working code, and gave designers a practical understanding of what code generation could do in their hands.

Legal and procurement were real constraints throughout. Graham navigated these by framing most early tools as temporary trials, which reduced the approval threshold and gave the team time to build genuine evidence of value. He managed vendor relationships actively, passing feedback from the team back to vendors directly and using those conversations to help improve their tools while securing early access and responsiveness in return.

Tools and how they were used

You.com

First tool adopted across the business. Aggregates multiple LLMs, selecting the best for each prompt. Structured training courses built in. The foundation for everything that followed.

Cursor

Code generation for designers. Engineering leaders ran the training. Designers moved from Figma to working prototypes without waiting for engineering resource. Demystified the design-to-code path.

Vercel V0

Experimented with as part of the guild's exploratory phase. Contributed to the team's understanding of AI-assisted front-end generation and where it added genuine value.

Lovable

Trialled alongside V0 as part of the rapid prototyping toolkit. Evaluated for fit with the team's workflow and design system standards.

Claude

Accounts seeded to the heaviest AI users in the team as a targeted trial. Used for research synthesis, requirements analysis, and interrogating vague briefs before committing to design work.

Figma Make

Extended to product managers as well as designers. PMs used it to prototype their intentions quickly, helping designers understand the thinking behind a brief without informing the final solution. Went down particularly well with PMs who were keen to engage visually but conscious of not stepping on design expertise.

The research Slack channel

Alongside the main guild channel, Graham set up a dedicated Slack channel for user research and opened it to the wider organisation. It surfaced researchers in pricing, marketing, and other functions who had been working in isolation. Pooling those people and their experience gave everyone better context on real customer problems, helped the group share approaches to AI-assisted research, and created shared understanding of where data was stored and how it should be handled.

Conferences and vendor demos

Graham encouraged guild members to attend conferences and events run by companies demonstrating their latest AI developments. The purpose was twofold: to build genuine excitement about the possibilities across the business, and to give Graham better intelligence about what the team saw as having real potential versus what looked impressive but would not deliver. It also helped test new tools and approaches against the specific culture and working context of Mimecast, which not every tool survived.

For vendor demos, Graham made a point of bringing people together across functions. He would typically invite someone from product management, someone from design, and someone from engineering to attend together. This was deliberate: shared exposure to new capability created shared context, and it meant that conversations about whether to trial a tool happened between the people who would actually use it, rather than being filtered through a single function's perspective.

During this period Graham was invited to join the AWS AI Trailblazers programme, a peer leadership forum for senior practitioners working through the real challenges of AI adoption. Conversations ranged across design, engineering, and product leadership, and included CEOs working through the same questions at an organisational level. The discussions around data sovereignty, regional data privacy regulation, and how to scale AI responsibly across international operations were directly relevant to the work Graham was leading at Mimecast, and fed back into how the guild approached tool trials and data handling questions. It was a useful reminder that the human and organisational challenges of AI were not unique to one company or one function.

04 The thinking
On human-centred AI

AI is most often framed as a technology problem. The real challenge is human. When AI removes the technical barriers between roles, human judgement becomes the bottleneck and individual blind spots become the critical weakness. Human-centred AI is about making that judgement sustainable: mitigating blind spots through cross-functional working, building the confidence and open critique that allows people to exercise genuine accountability for outcomes rather than just ownership of tasks.

This is what care capital means in practice. When people feel trusted and supported, they commit. When they commit, they take accountability. That is how cross-functional teams move from capability overlap to genuine shared ownership.

The guild was not a structure imposed from above. It grew from a shared enthusiasm for new technology and a genuine collective desire to improve things for customers. Designers, product managers, engineers, and customer-facing colleagues all wanted to try new ideas and sample new ways of working. The guild gave that energy a home and a direction without trying to prescribe what the outcomes should be. It was deliberately open, exploratory, and willing to abandon what did not work.

One of the clearest practical results was a shift in how designers and product managers worked together. When PMs could prototype their intentions quickly in Figma Make, designers could understand the thinking behind a brief far more clearly. PMs were conscious that they did not want to determine the final solution, but the visual exercise helped them work through their own thinking and helped designers engage with the intent rather than interpreting a written specification. That kind of shared exploration removed a layer of friction that had existed since the start.

05 What changed
⚡ Design velocity

Designers moving from concept to working prototype faster than at any previous point. Cursor and Figma Make compressed the gap between intent and implementation on both sides of the design-engineering relationship.

🔬 Research at speed

AI-assisted synthesis reducing the time between fieldwork and insight. The research Slack channel pooled expertise from across functions, improving the quality of the questions being asked as well as the speed of the answers.

🤝 Cross-functional understanding

PMs prototyping their intentions in Figma Make gave designers clearer briefs and gave PMs a way to work through their thinking visually. Both sides found it valuable for different reasons.

🧲 Design system contribution

AI used to generate an adaptive dark mode palette for Mimecast Elements, dynamic rather than hard-coded, scalable across the system. A concrete output that came directly from the guild's experimental working practices.

🎯 Sharper requirements

Designers using AI to interrogate vague briefs before committing to design work. Coaching and prioritising their own time rather than responding reactively to whatever came in first.

🏛 Organisational readiness

When Mimecast committed to an AI-first operating model, the design team was already practising it. The guild made adoption a continuity, not a disruption.

This case study describes a team operating model and AI adoption programme developed at Mimecast, 2020–2025. The guild was open to all functions across the business, not limited to the design and research team.
