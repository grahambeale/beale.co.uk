Research & Strategy Yell · 2017

Project Alpha:
Rebuilding Yell's
consumer proposition

From directory to marketplace, a research-led strategic pivot

Scope Research · Strategy · Prototype validation

01 The situation

Yell was losing ground on multiple fronts simultaneously. Consumer NPS on the core search product sat at -1, monitored continuously via Mouseflow. Call volumes were down 39% year on year. Click-throughs to business listings had fallen 26%. Google's algorithm changes were degrading organic search traffic that accounted for over 90% of useful yell.com usage.

At the same time, focused vertical competitors, Rated People, Checkatrade, MyBuilder, were taking direct aim at Yell's heartland trades categories, all running higher direct traffic rates than Yell itself. The business had responded by doubling down on its existing advertising model. That model was running out of road.

"We must develop an entirely new consumer value proposition."

Internal strategic assessment, Yell 2017
02 The decision

The CTO had grown frustrated with the organisation's difficulty seeing beyond its current business model. After a conversation with him, Graham initiated a research and validation programme to determine whether a fundamentally different consumer proposition was viable. The question was not how to improve the existing product. It was whether a new one was needed entirely.

The proposed direction, a two-sided marketplace built on trust signals, rich messaging, and co-created quoting, sat well outside business as usual. It did not immediately resonate internally, and there was no early commitment. The advertising model was the organisation's revenue engine, and a marketplace proposition that bypassed it was not an easy sell.

The research would need to be rigorous enough to take a contrary recommendation to the CEO, CTO, and CPO and make it stick.

03 The approach

Graham sourced, hired, and briefed Nomensa as the research partner for the programme, working closely with Hannah Tempest as lead consultant. Together they ran a six-week programme combining three evidence streams.

🗺 Competitive analysis

12 trade-focused vertical players mapped across proposition, traffic patterns, and user behaviour.

📊 NPS survey

May 2017 NPS survey data revealing user demographics, intent patterns, and satisfaction drivers.

📱 Prototype testing

12 participants across two user groups, consumers and tradespeople, tested against interactive Proto.io prototypes.

What the evidence showed

The competitive analysis confirmed that Rated People, Checkatrade, and MyBuilder were drawing direct traffic at rates Yell could not match. The existing search-and-list model had no structural advantage against them.

The NPS data revealed something more telling: 50% of consumers visiting yell.com already had someone in mind. They were not searching, they were looking up. The platform was not creating new relationships between consumers and tradespeople. It was facilitating lookups it could not own.

Prototype testing explored five themes: trust signals, chat-based co-creation of quotes, location and availability, filler jobs for tradespeople, and local recommendation networks. Both groups responded strongly to trust and messaging features. The co-created quote was the clearest moment of shared value, the point at which both sides got what they needed.

"Saves you a lot of time versus Google, all that research, waiting for people to get back to you."

Research participant, consumer group

Sam Harman, Head of Engineering, was a close partner throughout. His involvement ensured the concept was grounded in what could actually be built, and he later identified Sendbird as the messaging infrastructure the platform would use. Nick Butt and David Hughes also supported the initiative as it moved toward a build decision.

04 The recommendation

Graham presented the findings to the CEO, CTO, and CPO as a formal decision with three options: pursue Project Alpha, integrate elements into the existing product, or stop. The recommendation was to pursue. The business approved it.

The approval did not come quickly. The idea had taken time to gain traction in an organisation where this direction had not been the plan. The evidence made it harder to argue for the status quo, but it still required a decision to move against the grain of an existing, profitable model.

05 What happened

Graham left Yell before the build began. At least six months after his departure the engineering programme got underway, led by Sam Harman's team with Keith Merriman as Product Owner.

Platform outcomes

The platform went from beta to scale in nine months, built on Sendbird as the messaging layer. It generated thousands of consumer leads at an average job value of £200. Touchpoint NPS reached 47.

A platform designed from the ground up to create relationships, not lookups, delivered the satisfaction score the original product never could.

Outcome metrics corroborated by Keith Merriman, Product Owner on the delivery programme.

Project Alpha was a formal strategic initiative presented to Yell's CEO, CTO, and CPO in July 2017. Outcomes corroborated by Keith Merriman, Product Owner, and Sam Harman, Head of Engineering.
